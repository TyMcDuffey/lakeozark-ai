How much does an AI consultant cost?

A 2026 guide for small businesses. The honest answer is "it depends," but it doesn't have to be a mystery. Here's how AI consultants actually price their work, what's reasonable to pay, and how to tell good value from an expensive mistake.

By Ty McDuffey, J.D.  ·  June 2026  ·  6 min read

If you've started pricing out AI help for your business, you've probably noticed the quotes are all over the map, from a few hundred dollars to tens of thousands. That's not because someone's ripping you off. It's because "AI consultant" covers wildly different things, from a freelancer writing you some prompts to a firm that builds and runs an entire automated system for you. The price follows the work.

This guide breaks down the three ways AI consultants charge, the real-world ranges for a small business in 2026, what actually moves the number, and the warning signs worth watching before you sign anything.

The three ways AI consultants charge

Almost every AI engagement is priced one of three ways. Knowing which one you're being offered tells you most of what you need to know.

1. Hourly

You pay for the consultant's time, commonly somewhere in the range of $100 to $300 per hour for small-business work, sometimes more for specialized or legal-adjacent expertise. Hourly can make sense for a quick advisory project or a one-off question. The catch is that you're carrying all the risk: if it takes longer than expected, you pay for it, and you have little incentive on their side to be efficient. For anything beyond a small, well-defined task, hourly tends to get unpredictable fast.

2. Project or fixed fee

You agree on a defined deliverable, building a specific automation, setting up a tool, integrating two systems, for one fixed price. For small-business projects, this commonly lands anywhere from roughly $2,000 to $15,000 or more, depending entirely on scope. This is usually fairer than hourly because the price is known up front and the risk of overruns sits with the consultant. The thing to watch: a fixed-fee project often ends at "handoff." You get the thing, and then keeping it working is your problem.

3. Managed or monthly (done-for-you)

This is the model built for businesses that want the result, not a science project. You pay a one-time fee to build the system, then a monthly fee to run, monitor, and improve it, commonly $500 to $5,000+ per month depending on how much it's doing. It costs more over time than a one-off project, but it's the only model where someone is actually accountable for the system continuing to work after launch. For most small businesses, that ongoing accountability is the whole point of hiring out instead of doing it themselves.

What actually drives the price

Two businesses can get quotes that differ by 10x for reasons that have nothing to do with anyone being greedy. Here's what moves the number:

  • Scope. Automating one task is cheap. Automating your whole intake-to-follow-up pipeline is not. Most of the price is just how much work you're handing off.
  • Integrations. A standalone tool is simple. Wiring AI into your existing CRM, phone system, booking platform, or case-management software takes real work and pushes the price up.
  • Compliance and risk. If you're a law firm, a medical practice, or anyone handling sensitive data, doing it right, zero-retention configuration, written policies, documentation, costs more than a casual setup, and it should.
  • One-time vs. ongoing. A build is a one-time number. Running and improving it every month is a recurring one. Make sure you know which you're being quoted.
  • Who's doing it. A generalist freelancer, an overseas team, and a specialist who understands your industry will quote very differently, and the cheapest hour is not always the cheapest outcome.

Why the cheapest option is often the most expensive

It's tempting to grab the lowest quote, or to skip consultants entirely and build it yourself with a $20 subscription. Sometimes that works. Often it doesn't, and here's the pattern we see: the tool gets set up, it works for a few weeks, then something changes, it breaks, and there's no one to call. The "savings" turn into lost leads, frustrated customers, and your own time spent babysitting software you didn't want to manage in the first place. If a system isn't reliable, its low price is irrelevant, because you'll stop trusting it and go back to doing the work by hand.

Red flags when hiring an AI consultant

  • They can't explain it in plain English. If every answer is buzzwords, that's a problem. You should understand what you're paying for.
  • No mention of your data. Anyone responsible should bring up data retention and privacy before you do, especially if you handle client or customer information.
  • They disappear after launch. Ask directly: who keeps this working in three months? If the answer is "you," price it accordingly.
  • Vague scope, hourly billing. An open-ended project on an hourly meter is how budgets quietly triple.
  • No honest "maybe not." A consultant who says AI is the answer to everything isn't being straight with you. Sometimes it isn't worth it, and a good one will tell you.
