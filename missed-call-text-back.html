Contractors · Guide

Stop losing jobs to voicemail.

When you're on a ladder, under a sink, or running a saw, you can't answer the phone. The problem is that the customer calling rarely leaves a voicemail. They just call the next name on the list. Missed-call text-back is the simplest fix in the trades, and it's probably costing you more to ignore than to solve.

By Ty McDuffey, J.D.  ·  June 2026  ·  6 min read

Every contractor knows the feeling of seeing a missed call you couldn't get to, calling back an hour later, and hearing "oh, I already found somebody." That is not bad luck. That is the default outcome when a busy homeowner hits your voicemail, and it happens far more than most owners realize. The fix is cheap, it runs itself, and it plugs what is probably the biggest quiet leak in your business.

Why a missed call is usually a lost customer

Homeowners with a problem are not patient. A leak, a dead AC unit, a deck they want quoted, they want an answer now, and if they reach voicemail, they keep dialing down the list. Industry research on home-services calls has repeatedly found that a large share of inbound calls go unanswered, and that each missed call can represent a meaningful amount of lost revenue. Estimates vary, and one widely cited figure puts the value of a single missed home-services call in the four figures. You do not need the exact number to feel it in your own business: every call that rolls to voicemail is a coin flip you usually lose.

What missed-call text-back actually is

It is exactly what it sounds like. The instant a call you couldn't answer ends, the caller automatically gets a friendly text from your business. Something like: "Hi, this is Mike with Lakeside Plumbing, sorry I missed you, I'm on a job right now. What do you need? I can help you right here." Now, instead of dialing your competitor, that customer is in a text conversation with you, and you can reply between tasks. That one message turns a dead end into a live lead, and it takes you ten seconds instead of a callback you might not make until the job is gone.

Go beyond the text-back

The automatic text is step one. A good setup does more:

  • Qualifies the lead by asking a couple of quick questions: what's the job, where are you, when do you need it.
  • Captures their info so nothing gets lost in a sea of texts and sticky notes.
  • Drafts a quote or books the estimate so you're not starting from scratch when you finally sit down.
  • Follows up if they go quiet, because most jobs are won on the second or third touch, not the first.

Take it a step further and an AI receptionist can even answer simple calls live and book the appointment, so some of those calls never get missed in the first place.

The after-hours multiplier

A big share of service calls come in during evenings and weekends, which is exactly when you are off the clock and a voicemail is most likely. That is when text-back earns its keep. While your competitors' phones ring into the void after 5pm, yours is quietly booking Monday morning's work. For a lot of trades, the after-hours window is where the easiest wins are hiding.

What it does not do

It does not replace you. You still do the work, run the quotes, and make the calls that matter. It just makes sure the phone stops being a hole in the bottom of the bucket. Think of it like never letting a customer hit a busy signal again. The work is still yours. The leads just stop walking out the door while your hands are full.

Getting started

Start small. Turn on missed-call text-back and a simple after-hours auto-response. That alone plugs the biggest hole. Once you see the leads start landing in a text thread instead of disappearing, add the qualifying questions, the scheduling, and the follow-up.
