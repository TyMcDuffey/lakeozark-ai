Is AI safe for client data?

A lawyer's guide to confidentiality, Model Rule 1.6, and ABA Formal Opinion 512. The short answer: it can be, and the real danger probably isn't what you think. The risk usually isn't AI itself. It's how it's being used down the hall.

By Ty McDuffey, J.D.  ·  June 2026  ·  7 min read

Here's the uncomfortable reality at most firms: while leadership debates whether AI is safe, associates and staff are already using it. They're pasting a client's facts into a free chatbot to draft a demand letter or summarize a transcript, because it saves an hour. The efficiency is real. So is the exposure, and that gap between unofficial use and official policy is exactly where confidentiality problems start.

So let's answer the question directly. Is AI safe for confidential client data? It can be, when it's set up correctly. It is not when client information is dropped into consumer tools that may retain it or train on it. The difference between those two scenarios is configuration, documentation, and supervision, not the technology itself.

What your duty actually requires

Start with Model Rule 1.6. It requires a lawyer to make reasonable efforts to prevent the unauthorized disclosure of, or access to, information relating to the representation of a client. It does not say "never use new technology." It says be reasonable about protecting client information. The question is therefore not whether AI is allowed, but whether your use of it meets that reasonableness standard.

Then there's ABA Formal Opinion 512, issued July 29, 2024, the American Bar Association's first formal ethics guidance on generative AI. It confirms that lawyers may use these tools, and it lays out the duties that come with them: competence (understand the tool's benefits and risks), confidentiality, supervision of the work product, candor to the court, reasonable fees and billing, and, importantly, a duty to evaluate the AI vendors you rely on. Opinion 512 is the closest thing the profession has to a blueprint, and your own state's Rules of Professional Conduct sit on top of it.

The real risk: consumer chatbots, used casually

When people ask whether "AI" is safe, they're usually picturing the wrong threat. The danger isn't a properly configured system. It's the free, public chatbot that nobody at the firm decided to use but everyone is using anyway. Some consumer tools retain inputs and may use them to improve their models, which is the opposite of what confidentiality requires. Combine that with no firm policy, no record of what's being used, and the occasional fabricated citation, and you have a genuine problem, one that already has its own genre of news stories.

The fix is not to ban AI and fall behind. It's to replace the unofficial, risky version with an official, safe one.

What "safe" actually looks like

A defensible AI setup in a law firm has a handful of non-negotiable features:

  • Zero data retention. The tools are configured so that what you put in isn't stored or used to train any model. This is the single most important control, and it's available on the right platforms.
  • A written vendor agreement. The terms with your AI provider are documented, so the data handling isn't a guess, it's in writing. This is exactly the vendor vetting Opinion 512 calls for.
  • A firm AI use policy. A clear, written policy covering what may and may not be used with AI, how use is disclosed, and a decision tree for when not to use it at all.
  • Human supervision, always. AI drafts; a lawyer reviews and is responsible for every output. Nothing client-facing or court-facing goes out unreviewed.
  • Grounded drafting. Document workflows built on your own templates and materials, with verification steps, so fabricated cases and invented facts don't survive to a filing.
  • Documentation for audit. If the bar ever asks how your firm uses AI, you have the answer in writing before the question comes.

Notice that none of those are about being clever with technology. They're about discipline, the same kind of discipline you already apply to client files and trust accounts.

A practical checklist before you use any AI tool

  • Does this tool offer zero data retention, and is it turned on?
  • Do I have a written record of how the vendor handles our data?
  • Does my firm have an AI use policy that staff have actually read?
  • Is a lawyer reviewing every output before it leaves the firm?
  • Is there a clear list of what we will never put into AI?
  • Could I explain our AI use to a client, or to the bar, without flinching?

If you can't check all six, the answer to "is this safe?" is "not yet."
