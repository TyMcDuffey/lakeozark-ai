Surviving the Lake's seasonal swing.

Every Lake of the Ozarks business lives the same curve: a year's worth of revenue compressed into about twenty weekends, then a long quiet stretch where the phone barely rings, but the bills still do. Automation is unusually well suited to that curve, because it scales up for free in July and never draws a paycheck in January.

By Ty McDuffey, J.D.  ·  June 2026  ·  6 min read

If you run a business at the Lake, nobody has to explain the swing to you. Memorial Day to Labor Day is a sprint where every system you have gets stress-tested at once. Then the crowds go home, half the help goes back to school, and you spend the off-season doing the books, fixing what broke, and trying to turn winter inquiries into spring revenue. The swing is the defining fact of Lake business, and most of the standard advice (hire more, work more) just makes the curve more expensive.

The summer problem: capacity you can't hire

Summer's pain isn't a mystery: there is more of everything, calls, messages, bookings, reviews, walk-ins, than your team can touch. Seasonal hiring helps but it's a treadmill: hard to find, expensive to train, gone by September, and the new hire is the least equipped person to answer your phones in your busiest month. Meanwhile the overflow lands on the owner, which is how Lake operators end up answering messages from the dock at 10pm in July.

This is where automation changes the math, because the overflow is mostly routine. Hours, availability, pricing, booking, directions, status: the same questions a thousand times. An AI receptionist and messaging system absorbs that layer at any volume, instantly, around the clock. It doesn't get overwhelmed on the Fourth of July weekend, it doesn't quit in August, and it answers the 10pm call you physically can't. Your human team, whatever its size, spends its hours on the work that actually needs humans.

The off-season problem: presence without payroll

The off-season failure mode is the opposite: the business goes quiet in ways customers can feel. Calls ring out because the office is down to one person, winter hours confuse everybody, reviews sit unanswered, and the inquiries that do come in, next summer's weddings, slip rentals, projects, estimates, are the most valuable leads of the year landing when response is slowest.

Automation keeps the front door manned without keeping the lights on: every call answered or texted back, winter hours communicated consistently everywhere, inquiries captured and followed up, reviews acknowledged. Your business stays responsive twelve months a year while staffed for the season you're actually in. For trades, that's the winterization and remodel calls. For rentals, it's next season's bookings. For marine businesses, it's storage and off-season buyers.

The shoulder seasons: where the wins hide

April-May and September-October are when Lake businesses are won. The operators who respond fastest to early inquiries book the season first, and the ones still answering promptly in October pick up everything the closed-for-the-year competitors drop. A system that never changes its response time gives you both ends of that edge without you thinking about it.

What this looks like in practice

It is not one giant robot. It's a handful of unglamorous systems that compound: a phone that always answers or texts back, a booking flow that runs itself, review responses drafted for your approval, follow-up that doesn't forget, and the same answers given everywhere. Built once, around your business, and then it just runs, July and January alike.
