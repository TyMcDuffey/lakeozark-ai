AI for property managers and HOAs.

A property manager's inbox is a permanent flood: maintenance requests, owner questions, resident complaints, vendor coordination, board threads. Most of it is routine, time-sensitive, and relentless, which makes it the perfect candidate for automation. Here's what AI can take off the desk in a managed community at the Lake.

By Ty McDuffey, J.D.  ·  June 2026  ·  6 min read

The Lake of the Ozarks has an unusual amount of managed property for a market its size: condo and villa communities, HOAs, resort developments like the Village of Four Seasons, and thousands of second homes whose owners live hours away. That mix produces a communication load most managers know too well, the same questions and requests arriving by phone, email, and text, all day, from owners who expect a quick answer because they're paying dues for exactly that.

Where a manager's day actually goes

Strip a property manager's week down and an enormous share of it is repeated communication: When is the pool opening? Who do I call about my dock? My AC is out, my renter says the code doesn't work, did my dues payment go through, can I get a copy of the bylaws, when is the next board meeting. Each message is reasonable on its own. Together they bury the actual work of managing property, vendor oversight, budgets, inspections, board strategy, under a mountain of inbox.

What AI handles well in a managed community

Maintenance request intake and routing

AI can take a maintenance request the way a good coordinator would: capture the unit, the issue, photos, access instructions, and urgency, then log it and route it to the right vendor or staffer instantly, at 2pm or 2am. Residents get an immediate confirmation instead of a voicemail, and nothing falls into the gap between channels.

Owner and resident questions

The recurring questions, amenities, hours, rules, fees, documents, meeting dates, follow a script that AI answers instantly and consistently across phone, email, and text. Distant owners, a huge share at the Lake, get the responsiveness they're paying for without a human retyping the same answer for the hundredth time.

Rental messaging in mixed communities

Many Lake associations and managers also juggle short-term rentals in their buildings, which adds guest messaging to the pile. The same systems we describe in our vacation rental automation guide fold into a property-management setup, so guest questions stop landing on the association's office line.

Board and owner reporting

Monthly summaries, request-volume reports, open-item lists, and meeting-prep documents can be drafted automatically from the activity the system already tracks, your review, the machine's first draft. Boards get professional, consistent reporting; you get hours back every month.

What stays human

Judgment calls, disputes, violations, emergencies, and anything with legal or financial weight stays with people, full stop. An angry owner, a flooded unit, a contested fine, those need a human, and a good system routes them to one immediately with full context instead of pretending to handle them. The automation exists to clear the routine layer so your people have time for exactly these moments.

The accountability angle

Managed communities run on trust, and boards ask hard questions about new systems. Everything we build is documented: what the AI handles, what it escalates, where the data lives, and how privacy is protected, in writing, suitable for handing to a board. That's the standard we apply to law firms, and a community's data deserves the same care.
